BookDB

A recommendation engine for readers, wrapped in a small social platform: shelve what you've read, rate it, follow people with taste like yours, and get pointed at the next book you won't be able to put down. I rebuilt it from scratch because it's a full recommender and the social graph that feeds it — the two halves that make suggestions feel personal.

What it is

Your next read, from kindred readers

The core idea is collaborative filtering: people who agreed with you in the past are a good guide to what you'll like next. BookDB learns the latent "taste" behind each reader and each book, places them in the same space, and recommends the titles whose taste-vector sits closest to yours. No genre tags required — the patterns emerge from ratings alone.

Around that engine sits a social layer: shelves, ratings, follows and reviews. It's not decoration — it's the data source. Every interaction is a signal, and a recommender is only as good as the behaviour it gets to learn from. Building both halves is what makes the loop real.

Architecture

How a recommendation is made

From a reader's history to a ranked shelf, the path is consistent:

  1. Collect

    Gather the reader's ratings, shelves and follows from the platform.

  2. Factorise

    Learn latent factors for readers and books from the ratings matrix.

  3. Score

    Predict the reader's affinity for every unread book.

  4. Rank & blend

    Order by predicted score, blending in social and content signals.

  5. Serve

    Return the ranked recommendations through the API and learn from what they pick.

Reflection

What rebuilding it taught me

  • Latent factors are magic you can derive. Matrix factorisation invents "taste dimensions" nobody labelled — and they work.
  • Cold start is the real-world problem. The maths is clean; handling the reader or book with no history is where products live or die.
  • The social layer is the dataset. Recommendations are downstream of behaviour — building the platform that creates the signal is half the engine.
  • Sparsity dominates. Almost no one has rated almost any book; every design choice is shaped by that emptiness.
  • Rank, don't predict. Users never see a rating prediction — they see an ordered list, so ranking metrics are the ones that matter.
